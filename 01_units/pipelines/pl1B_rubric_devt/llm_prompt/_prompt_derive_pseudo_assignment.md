#### Section A — Scenario framing and tension declaration  
**Target:** ~90–110 words  

1. **System identification** (1–2 sentences, max 35 words)  
   Name the sociotechnical system and institutional setting.

2. **Primary tension declaration** (1 sentence, max 30 words)  
   Identify one structured interaction between two institutional demands described in the case and state how that interaction is operationalised within the workflow.

3. **Scope declaration** (2 bullet points, max 20 words each)  
   - **In scope:** Institutional demands and interaction mechanisms described in the case.  
   - **Out of scope or deferred:** Outcomes, fairness, effectiveness, redesign, or broader social consequences.

4. **Boundary statement** (1 sentence, max 25 words)  
   State one analytic limit that constrains interpretation of the identified interaction.

Do not evaluate the tension.  

Do not infer consequences not described.

You will likely need to stay below individual maxima to remain within the section target.
#### Section B — Constraint interaction claims  
**Target:** ~120–150 words total  

Provide three analytic claims using the fixed template below.  

Each claim must identify an interaction between two institutional demands explicitly named in the case or directly evidenced by described governance rules, scoring procedures, or scheduling structures.

Note: Tools (e.g., AI-generated summaries, structured assessments) are mechanisms within the workflow and are not institutional demands.
#### Claim template (repeat three times)
**Claim statement** (aim 35–45 words; max 50 words only if necessary):  

> In this system, **\[institutional demand A\]** interacts with **\[institutional demand B\]** through **\[mechanism/tool\]**, shaping **\[workflow stage or role\]** by **\[structural effect\]**.

**Field guidance**
- **\[institutional demand A\]**, **\[institutional demand B\]**:  
  e.g., published evaluation criteria · throughput expectations · documentation requirements · human verification requirement · reporting obligations

- **\[mechanism/tool\]**:  
  Must be directly described in the case as part of the workflow or system configuration (e.g., AI-generated summary · structured preliminary assessment · reviewer assignment logic · scoring rubric · scheduling structure).
  
- **\[workflow stage or role\]**:  
  e.g., preliminary screening · reviewer preparation · committee deliberation · documentation stage

- **\[structural effect\]** (verb phrase):  
  e.g., sequencing attention · redistributing visibility · structuring documentation · narrowing discretionary exploration · formalising review order

**Constraint checks**
- Each claim must involve two distinct institutional demands.  
- Do not introduce demands not described in the case.  
- Do not discuss outcomes, merit, fairness, or impact.  
- Do not argue for change.
- Do not introduce institutional demands not named or evidenced in the case.
#### Section C — Responsibility under layered constraints  
**Target:** ~125–155 words  

1. **Layered responsibility analysis** (2 short paragraphs, max 65 words each):  
   - Paragraph 1: Identify where institutional requirements are embedded in workflow stages or system components in ways that structure or constrain action.
   - Paragraph 2: Identify where reliance or dependency appears across roles and tools.

2. **Diffusion statement** (1 sentence, max 25 words):  
   State where responsibility cannot be located in a single role because action is mediated across institutional layers.

Do not assign blame.  
Do not assess adequacy of oversight.
#### Section D — Institutional demands shaping action  
**Target:** ~120–150 words  

List three institutional demands explicitly named in the case or directly evidenced by described governance rules, scoring procedures, or scheduling structures. Format each entry as:

- **Institutional demand** (max 20 words)  
- **What it shapes** (choose one): authority · timing · visibility · sequencing · documentation  
- **Structural effect** (1 sentence, max 30 words)

Each demand must be directly grounded in the case description.  
Do not introduce new institutional logics not evidenced in the scenario.  
Do not introduce justice claims, equity analysis, or evaluative language.
#### Section E — Operational tension (descriptive only)  
**Target:** ~80–100 words  

- Identify one recurring interaction between institutional demands (max 25 words).  
- Describe how the system operationalises that interaction (1–2 sentences, max 50 words).  
- State what remains structurally unresolved within the described workflow (1 sentence, max 25 words).

Do not resolve the interaction.  
Do not speculate about consequences beyond the case description.
#### Section F — Analytic limits under constraint  
**Target:** ~95–115 words  

Provide three limit statements, each formatted as:
- “This analysis cannot determine whether … because …” (max 30 words)

Each limit must relate specifically to:
- institutional interaction,
- responsibility distribution, or
- constraint layering described in the case.

Then add one closing sentence (max 25 words) explaining why maintaining non-resolution is analytically necessary at this stage.

Do not propose reform, investigation, or policy change.---

### Case AP2 — AI-Assisted Grant Application Screening
#### Your role and analytic task
You are a newly appointed software developer working within the municipal community grants office’s digital systems team. The office administers small competitive grants to neighbourhood organisations for projects such as after-school programs, community garden initiatives, tenant support workshops, and local food distribution efforts.

The municipality operates its grant screening and review systems in-house. Your team maintains the grant management platform, including the eligibility verification workflow, reviewer assignment logic, scoring interfaces, documentation records, and the AI-assisted preliminary screening component.

Before contributing to future system updates, you are asked to understand how the current adjudication workflow operates and how institutional demands are embedded within system configuration.

Your task is not to assess whether the tool improves funding outcomes, nor to recommend changes. Instead, you are asked to analyse how responsibility, authority, sequencing, and documentation are structured across workflow stages, software components, and governance requirements.

The focus of your analysis is how institutional demands interact within the system as currently designed.
#### Public grant funding context
The agency administers competitive funding programs under publicly published evaluation criteria. These criteria define program objectives, eligibility requirements, and structured assessment categories.

For example, evaluation criteria for a community development grant may include:

- demonstrated alignment with the program mission (e.g., strengthening neighbourhood capacity),
- clarity and feasibility of the proposed activities,
- evidence of organisational capacity to deliver the project,
- proposed budget realism and cost justification,
- anticipated community reach within the defined program scope.

These criteria are translated into scoring categories within a structured rubric used by reviewers. These categories are represented as structured data fields within the grant management system

Funding decisions must be documented and justified in accordance with internal governance rules and external accountability obligations tied to public funding mandates.

Applications are submitted through a digital portal and reviewed in stages. Review committees retain formal decision-making authority.
#### Scope of this scenario
For the purposes of this scenario, the system should be treated as an internal administrative workflow that supports grant evaluation. The analytic focus is not on whether particular projects deserve funding, nor on the broader social impact of grant allocation.
The focus is on how the agency operationalises preliminary screening and review under layered institutional demands.
#### Public grant funding and adjudication process

The municipal grants office administers competitive funding rounds on an annual cycle. Applications are submitted through a digital portal and proceed through a structured adjudication process consisting of:

1. **Eligibility verification**  
   Administrative staff confirm that applications meet basic program requirements (e.g., applicant type, submission completeness, required documentation). Applications that do not meet eligibility requirements are recorded as ineligible and removed from further review within the funding round. Applications that meet eligibility proceed to detailed assessment.

2. **Preliminary screening, categorisation, and reviewer assignment**  
   Eligible applications are categorised according to program streams and assessment areas defined in the published criteria. At this stage, applications are assigned to individual reviewers based on committee composition, declared areas of expertise, and workload distribution rules. Files are organised and scheduled for review meetings within the funding cycle.

3. **Individual reviewer assessment and scoring**  
   Assigned reviewers evaluate applications against published criteria using a structured scoring rubric. Reviewers provide numerical scores for defined assessment categories and submit written justifications aligned to those categories.

4. **Committee deliberation and recommendation**  
   Reviewers’ scores and written assessments are compiled for committee discussion. The committee reviews comparative scoring patterns, discusses applications, and formulates funding recommendations.

5. **Documentation and reporting**  
   Final decisions are recorded with written justification aligned to published criteria, scoring records, and reporting requirements.

Review committees retain formal decision-making authority, and all applications that pass eligibility verification proceed through scoring and committee deliberation.
#### AI-assisted preliminary screening (within Stage 2)

During the preliminary screening, categorisation, and reviewer assignment stage, an AI tool generates:

- a condensed summary of each application,
- highlighted excerpts linked to published evaluation criteria,
- a structured preliminary assessment aligned with program streams and rubric categories.

The tool does not determine eligibility, generate or assign scores, or issue funding recommendations, and it is not incorporated into the scoring rubric. All applications that pass eligibility verification proceed to individual scoring and committee deliberation.

The AI-generated outputs are used as part of file preparation. They support categorisation into program streams and provide structured information that administrative staff use when preparing reviewer assignments, and structure how application materials are displayed when reviewers first open a file.

Applications are scheduled for review meetings according to established workload distribution rules and funding-cycle timelines. The AI-generated preliminary assessment may be visible at the outset of review, but reviewers retain responsibility for evaluating the full application and applying the scoring rubric independently.

The AI tool therefore shapes how information is organised, presented, and sequenced within Stage 2, without determining scoring outcomes or final decisions.
#### Human review and committee deliberation (Stages 3-5)

Individual reviewers examine both the AI-generated outputs and the full application documents. Reviewers apply the published scoring rubric, assign numerical scores within defined assessment categories, and provide written justifications aligned to those categories.

Reviewers are formally instructed to base their scoring and written assessments on the complete application materials. AI-generated summaries may be visible at the outset of review but do not replace independent application of the rubric.

During committee deliberation, individual scores and written assessments are compiled for comparison. The committee discusses scoring patterns, considers alignment with published criteria, and formulates funding recommendations. Recommendations are recorded alongside scoring records and written justifications.
#### Institutional context and layered demands
The grants office operates under multiple institutional demands, including:

- commitment to advancing the stated program mission (e.g., strengthening neighbourhood capacity and community wellbeing),
- adherence to publicly published evaluation criteria,
- timeliness expectations tied to fixed funding-cycle schedules,
- documentation and record-keeping standards supporting audit and public reporting requirements,
- administrative workload distribution requirements tied to reviewer capacity and committee structure,
- formal retention of human scoring and decision authority.

The program mission defines the substantive objectives the funding round is intended to support. The published criteria translate those objectives into assessable categories within the scoring rubric. The published criteria structure scoring categories, while the program mission frames the broader substantive objectives those categories are intended to advance. The materials provided here describe workflow stages, tool outputs, reviewer responsibilities, scoring procedures, and governance requirements. They do not specify:

- how frequently reviewers depart from AI-generated structuring,
- whether review depth varies across applications,
- how comparative scoring translates into final funding allocations.
